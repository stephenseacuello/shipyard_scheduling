\begin{abstract}
%==============================================================================
Reinforcement learning (RL) has achieved impressive results across many domains, yet suffers a critical failure mode in \textit{hierarchical scheduling problems with action masking}: \textbf{entropy collapse}. When the set of valid actions is dynamically constrained to few options, policy entropy is mathematically bounded, gradients vanish, and RL algorithms converge to deterministic, suboptimal policies. We identify this phenomenon, provide theoretical analysis of entropy bounds under masking, and validate across three domains: shipyard scheduling (PPO entropy: 2.27 $\to$ 0.00 by epoch 5), job shop scheduling (JSSP), and vehicle routing with time windows (VRPTW).

We demonstrate that imitation learning via Dataset Aggregation (DAgger) avoids entropy collapse entirely, achieving \textbf{97.0\% $\pm$ 2.5\% of expert throughput} on HD Hyundai Heavy Industries (HHI) Ulsan shipyard---the world's largest shipbuilding facility with 200 blocks, 10 dry docks, and 9 Goliath cranes---compared to 0.4\% for PPO and 28.7\% for SAC. Our 10-seed validation with Bayesian hyperparameter sweep (11 trials) identifies optimal configurations: hidden\_dim=64, lr=0.008, 5 iterations. We further extend the framework with an integrated \textbf{supply chain module} modeling multi-supplier procurement, material inventory with stockout risk, and labor pool allocation with skill matching---adding 3 node types and 6 edge types to the heterogeneous GNN while maintaining full backward compatibility. Expert evaluation on the extended environment achieves 230.6 $\pm$ 5.5 blocks erected with active procurement management (36.6 orders, 34.8 deliveries per episode). The framework achieves 214 kHz inference throughput, enabling real-time deployment. We further introduce plate-level block decomposition with geometry-driven processing times derived from structural 3D model data, and benchmark a mixed-integer programming (MIP) baseline using PuLP CBC for systematic OR vs RL comparison. Curriculum learning with observation normalization partially closes the scalability gap, improving DAgger from 0\% to 34.3\% of expert on 200-block instances. Cross-instance scaling analysis across four configurations (10--1600 blocks) reveals that Expert (EDD) advantage grows with instance size, achieving 4.7$\times$ the throughput of MPC at 200 blocks. We provide theoretical bounds, multi-domain validation, and practical guidelines for practitioners facing hierarchical scheduling with constrained action spaces.

\vspace{0.5em}
\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} Entropy collapse, action masking, imitation learning, DAgger, reinforcement learning, graph neural networks, shipyard scheduling
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}
%==============================================================================

Deep reinforcement learning (RL) has achieved remarkable success across diverse domains, from game playing to robotic control. However, a critical failure mode emerges when RL is applied to \textit{hierarchical scheduling problems} with constrained action spaces: \textbf{entropy collapse}. In this work, we identify, analyze, and address this phenomenon, demonstrating that imitation learning provides a principled solution where standard RL approaches fail.

\subsection{The Problem: Entropy Collapse Under Action Masking}

Many real-world scheduling problems involve \textit{action masking}---dynamically constraining the set of valid actions based on system state. In vehicle dispatching, only idle vehicles can be assigned. In job shop scheduling, only operations with satisfied precedence constraints are executable. In crane scheduling, only ready blocks can be lifted.

When the number of valid actions $k(s)$ becomes small (often just 1-2 options), policy entropy is mathematically bounded:
\begin{equation}
H(\pi(\cdot|s)) \leq \log k(s)
\end{equation}

As training progresses, standard RL algorithms (PPO, SAC) converge to deterministic policies. Once entropy collapses, exploration ceases, gradients vanish for non-dominant actions, and the policy becomes trapped in suboptimal fixed points. We term this the \textbf{entropy collapse death spiral}.

\subsection{Empirical Evidence}

We validate entropy collapse across three scheduling domains (see Table~\ref{tab:entropy_collapse_validated}):

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Shipyard block scheduling}: PPO entropy collapses from 2.27 to 0.00 nats by epoch 5, achieving only 0.4\% of expert throughput
    \item \textbf{Job shop scheduling (JSSP)}: Entropy declines to $\sim$0.3 nats with degraded performance
    \item \textbf{Vehicle routing (VRPTW)}: Entropy is bottlenecked at 0.02 nats from initialization due to extremely constrained action space
\end{itemize}

Standard mitigation strategies---entropy bonuses, softened masking, adaptive temperature tuning---delay but do not prevent collapse. The fundamental issue is that policy gradient methods require exploration, but exploration is impossible when few actions are valid.

\subsection{The Solution: Imitation Learning}

We demonstrate that \textbf{Dataset Aggregation (DAgger)} avoids entropy collapse entirely by sidestepping the exploration problem:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{No exploration required}: Learning is supervised, using expert demonstrations
    \item \textbf{Dense feedback}: Every state receives a correct action label
    \item \textbf{Well-behaved gradients}: Cross-entropy loss maintains gradients regardless of action distribution
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Industrial Case Study: HHI Ulsan Shipyard}

We validate our approach on HD Hyundai Heavy Industries (HHI) Ulsan---the world's largest shipbuilding facility spanning 1,780 acres along 4 kilometers of coastline on Mipo Bay. The Ulsan shipyard produces LNG carriers with each ship comprising approximately 200 blocks weighing up to 900 tons. The facility operates 10 dry docks served by 9 Goliath cranes standing 109 meters tall. Figure~\ref{fig:shipyard_layout} shows the facility layout with production zones, and Figure~\ref{fig:workflow} illustrates the 11-stage production workflow.

Key dock specifications from published data~\cite{ship_tech_hhi}: Dock~3 (672m $\times$ 92m) is the world's largest, capable of simultaneously constructing vessels up to 1M~DWT with two Goliath cranes. The H-Dock (490m $\times$ 115m $\times$ 13.5m) is the world's first dry dock purpose-built for offshore vessels. Dock~1 (390m $\times$ 80m) specializes in LNG carriers, while Docks~8--9 (built 1996) handle VLCCs.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related_work}
%==============================================================================

\subsection{Shipyard Scheduling}

Shipyard scheduling has been studied extensively in the operations research literature. \citet{cho1999automated} presented early work on automated planning for shipyard operations, focusing on welding sequence optimization. \citet{lee1996spatial} developed an early spatial scheduling system (DAS-CURVE) for shipbuilding using constraint-based reasoning. \citet{seo2007block} applied case-based reasoning to block assembly planning, leveraging historical scheduling data.

More recently, \citet{kwon2016spatial} formulated large assembly block spatial scheduling as a mixed-integer program with precedence and spatial constraints. \citet{zheng2011spatial} proposed a spatial scheduling algorithm minimizing makespan at block assembly shops. \citet{qu2013integrated} integrated constraint reasoning with evolutionary methods for block assembly sequence planning.

Korean shipbuilding specifically has received attention due to its scale and efficiency. \citet{shin2008heuristic} applied differential evolution to spatial planning of assembly blocks with process schedules, while \citet{roh2007generation} addressed block erection simulation for process planning and scheduling. The vehicle routing component connects to the broader VRP literature surveyed by \citet{toth2014vehicle}, with SPMTs adding complexity through capacity constraints and multi-vehicle coordination.

\paragraph{Plate-Level Production Modeling.} At a finer granularity, several works have addressed plate-level decomposition and its impact on production scheduling. \citet{zhong2013assembly} proposed an assembly unit partitioning method for hull structures that maps 3D ship models into hierarchical plate-panel-block assemblies, enabling bottom-up production planning driven by structural geometry. \citet{liu2012scheduling} addressed dynamic block assembly scheduling through hybrid simulation and spatial optimization, explicitly modeling spatial constraints arising from block dimensions and facility layouts. \citet{lee2012block} developed a block spatial scheduling model applied to Korean shipyards, demonstrating that incorporating spatial and temporal constraints into mathematical formulations improves schedule makespan. Our work builds on these findings by integrating a plate-count processing time model into the RL environment, calibrated via per-stage regression coefficients from structural data.

\subsection{Reinforcement Learning for Scheduling}

Deep RL has achieved notable success in combinatorial optimization and scheduling. \citet{bello2017neural} applied policy gradient methods to the traveling salesman problem, demonstrating that neural networks can learn effective construction heuristics. \citet{kool2019attention} extended this with attention-based models, achieving state-of-the-art results on multiple routing problems. \citet{nazari2018reinforcement} applied RL specifically to vehicle routing with stochastic demands.

For job-shop scheduling, \citet{zhang2020learning} proposed a seminal graph-based RL approach using GNNs to encode machine and job states. \citet{park2021learning} combined heterogeneous GNNs with PPO and demonstrated transfer learning across problem sizes. \citet{wang2021dynamic} applied attention networks to flexible job-shop scheduling, outperforming traditional dispatching rules.

\subsection{Graph Neural Networks for Combinatorial Optimization}

GNNs have emerged as powerful tools for structured optimization problems. The foundational Graph Convolutional Network (GCN) architecture was introduced by \citet{kipf2017semi}. \citet{velickovic2018graph} proposed Graph Attention Networks (GAT) with learned attention weights for neighbor aggregation. For heterogeneous graphs, \citet{schlichtkrull2018modeling} developed Relational GCN for typed edges, while \citet{wang2019heterogeneous} and \citet{hu2020heterogeneous} extended attention mechanisms to heterogeneous information networks.

\citet{cappart2023combinatorial} provides a comprehensive survey of GNN applications in combinatorial optimization, establishing a taxonomy of problem representations and solution approaches.

\subsection{Predictive Health Management}

The integration of predictive maintenance into scheduling decisions is an emerging area. \citet{jardine2006review} reviews the foundations of condition-based maintenance including statistical degradation models. \citet{si2011remaining} surveys statistical approaches to remaining useful life (RUL) estimation, including the Wiener process models we employ. For deep learning approaches, \citet{zhu2019estimation} applied multi-scale CNNs to bearing RUL estimation.

\subsection{Multi-Agent and Hierarchical RL}

Multi-agent reinforcement learning has gained prominence for coordinated decision-making. \citet{lowe2017multi} introduced MADDPG for cooperative-competitive settings. \citet{yu2022surprising} demonstrated the surprising effectiveness of PPO in cooperative multi-agent games, inspiring our MAPPO implementation for SPMT coordination.

Hierarchical RL with the options framework \citep{sutton1999between} enables temporally extended actions. \citet{bacon2017option} proposed the option-critic architecture for end-to-end learning of options and termination conditions.

\subsection{Multi-Objective Optimization in RL}

Scalarization approaches for multi-objective RL are surveyed by \citet{hayes2022practical}. Chebyshev scalarization provides better Pareto coverage than linear weighting \citep{van2014multi}. Hypervolume-based methods \citep{zitzler2003performance} optimize the volume dominated by the Pareto front.

\subsection{Robust RL and Sim-to-Real Transfer}

Domain randomization \citep{tobin2017domain} varies environment parameters during training for sim-to-real transfer. Adversarial training \citep{pinto2017robust} trains against worst-case perturbations. These techniques are critical for deploying RL in real-world manufacturing settings.

\subsection{Integrated Production and Supply Chain Scheduling}

Joint optimization of production scheduling and supply chain management has received growing attention. \citet{mula2010mathematical} surveys mathematical programming formulations for production-supply chain integration. \citet{maravelias2012general} presents a general framework for integrating planning and scheduling with supply chain decisions. For RL-based approaches, \citet{hubbs2020or} demonstrates deep RL for inventory management with multi-echelon supply chains.

In shipbuilding specifically, material procurement and workforce allocation are tightly coupled with block production schedules. Late steel deliveries cascade into schedule delays across all downstream stages. Our supply chain extension models this coupling explicitly through heterogeneous graph edges connecting production blocks to inventory and labor nodes.

\paragraph{Positioning of This Work.} Our work differs from prior approaches by: (1) modeling large-scale commercial shipbuilding with 200 blocks per ship and 10 concurrent dry docks, (2) implementing a comprehensive suite of RL algorithms (PPO, SAC, MAPPO, Hierarchical RL), (3) developing edge-aware GNNs with temporal attention and hierarchical pooling, (4) comparing against strong OR baselines including MPC and GA schedulers, (5) addressing robustness through domain randomization and adversarial training, and (6) integrating supply chain management (multi-supplier procurement, inventory, labor) into the scheduling MDP via a backward-compatible heterogeneous graph extension.

\section{Methodology}
\label{sec:methodology}
%==============================================================================

\subsection{Problem Formulation}

We formulate HHI Ulsan shipyard scheduling as a Markov Decision Process (MDP) defined by the tuple $(\mathcal{S}, \mathcal{A}, P, R, \gamma)$ where $\mathcal{S}$ is the state space, $\mathcal{A}$ is the action space, $P$ is the transition function, $R$ is the reward function, and $\gamma \in [0, 1)$ is the discount factor.

\subsubsection{State Space}

The state $s_t \in \mathcal{S}$ is represented as a heterogeneous graph $G = (V, E)$ with six node types:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Block nodes} $V_b$: Each of the 200 blocks per ship has features including current production stage (0-10), block type (flat/curved), completion percentage, time to due date, predecessor completion status, weight (up to 900 tons), ship assignment, and erection sequence number.

    \item \textbf{SPMT nodes} $V_v$: Each vehicle $v_j$ has features for status (idle, traveling empty, traveling loaded, in maintenance), current load ratio, and three-component health vector (hydraulic, tires, engine).

    \item \textbf{Goliath crane nodes} $V_g$: Each of the 9 cranes has features for assigned dock, rail position (0-100\%), status, and three-component health vector (hoist, trolley, gantry).

    \item \textbf{Ship nodes} $V_s$: Each ship under construction has features for erection progress (0-100\%), blocks erected count, assigned dock, and status.

    \item \textbf{Dock nodes} $V_d$: Each of the 10 dry docks has features for occupancy, current ship (if any), and associated cranes.

    \item \textbf{Facility nodes} $V_f$: Each production facility has features for queue depth, utilization, and average wait time.
\end{itemize}

Edge types encode relationships:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $(b_i, \texttt{needs\_transport}, v_j)$: Block requires SPMT transport
    \item $(v_j, \texttt{can\_transport}, b_i)$: SPMT can serve block
    \item $(b_i, \texttt{needs\_lift}, g_k)$: Block requires Goliath crane lift for erection
    \item $(b_i, \texttt{at}, f_l)$: Block is at facility
    \item $(b_i, \texttt{precedes}, b_{i'})$: Erection precedence constraint
    \item $(b_i, \texttt{assigned\_to}, s_m)$: Block belongs to ship
    \item $(g_k, \texttt{serves}, d_n)$: Goliath crane assigned to dock
    \item $(s_m, \texttt{in\_dock}, d_n)$: Ship is in dry dock
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Action Space}

The action space $\mathcal{A}$ is hierarchical with four action types (simplified from dual-yard formulations by removing barge operations):

\begin{equation}
a_t = (\texttt{type}, \texttt{spmt\_idx}, \texttt{request\_idx}, \texttt{crane\_idx}, \texttt{lift\_idx}, \texttt{equip\_idx})
\end{equation}

Action types:
\begin{enumerate}
    \setcounter{enumi}{-1}
    \item Dispatch SPMT to fulfill transport request
    \item Dispatch Goliath crane to erect block in dock
    \item Trigger preventive maintenance on SPMT or crane
    \item Hold (no operation)
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection{Reward Function}

The reward function combines five components:

\begin{equation}
r_t = w_c \cdot \Delta_{\text{complete}} + w_s \cdot \Delta_{\text{ship}} - w_\tau \cdot \Delta_{\text{tardy}} - w_e \cdot \Delta_{\text{empty}} - w_b \cdot \Delta_{\text{breakdown}} - w_m \cdot \mathbb{1}_{\text{maint}}
\end{equation}

where $\Delta_{\text{ship}}$ rewards ship launches (all blocks erected), $w_c, w_s, w_\tau, w_e, w_b, w_m$ are weighting coefficients, and $\mathbb{1}_{\text{maint}}$ is an indicator for maintenance actions.

\paragraph{Incremental Tardiness Calculation.}
We compute tardiness incrementally to prevent reward explosion. For each tardy block (i.e., where $t_{\text{sim}} > t_{\text{due}}$), we add $\Delta t$ to the total tardiness at each simulation step, rather than accumulating the full $(t_{\text{sim}} - t_{\text{due}}) \cdot \Delta t$. This linear accumulation ensures bounded rewards:
\begin{equation}
\Delta_{\text{tardy}} = \sum_{b \in \mathcal{B}_{\text{incomplete}}} \mathbb{1}[t_{\text{sim}} > t_{\text{due}}^b] \cdot \Delta t
\end{equation}

\subsection{Equipment Degradation Model}

We model equipment health using a Wiener process with load-dependent drift. For SPMTs:

\begin{equation}
H_{t+\Delta t}^{\text{SPMT}} = H_t - \mu_{\text{SPMT}}(L_t, W_t) \Delta t + \sigma \sqrt{\Delta t} \cdot \epsilon_t
\end{equation}

where $\mu_{\text{SPMT}}(L_t, W_t) = \mu_0 (1 + \alpha L_t + \beta W_t)$ depends on load ratio $L_t$ and block weight $W_t$ (normalized to 900 tons).

For Goliath cranes:

\begin{equation}
H_{t+\Delta t}^{\text{Goliath}} = H_t - \mu_{\text{crane}}(W_t, C_t) \Delta t + \sigma \sqrt{\Delta t} \cdot \epsilon_t
\end{equation}

where $\mu_{\text{crane}}(W_t, C_t) = \mu_0 (1 + \gamma W_t + \delta C_t)$ depends on block weight and lift cycle count $C_t$.

Equipment fails when health drops below threshold $H_{\text{fail}} = 20$. Remaining useful life is estimated as:

\begin{equation}
\text{RUL}(H_t) = \frac{H_t - H_{\text{fail}}}{\mu(\bar{L})}
\end{equation}

\subsection{GNN Encoder Architecture}

The GNN encoder processes the heterogeneous graph through $L$ message passing layers. For each node type $\tau$ and layer $l$:

\begin{equation}
h_i^{(l+1)} = \sigma \left( W_\tau^{(l)} h_i^{(l)} + \sum_{r \in \mathcal{R}} \sum_{j \in \mathcal{N}_r(i)} \alpha_{ij}^r W_r^{(l)} h_j^{(l)} \right)
\end{equation}

where $\mathcal{R}$ is the set of edge types, $\mathcal{N}_r(i)$ are neighbors connected by edge type $r$, $\alpha_{ij}^r$ are attention coefficients, and $\sigma$ is a nonlinearity.

\subsubsection{Edge-Aware Encoding}

We extend the GNN with edge features capturing operational relationships:

\begin{equation}
e_{ij} = [\text{travel\_time}_{ij}, \text{capacity\_ratio}_{ij}, \text{urgency}_{ij}]
\end{equation}

The edge-aware message passing becomes:

\begin{equation}
m_{ij}^{(l)} = \phi_e(h_i^{(l)}, h_j^{(l)}, e_{ij})
\end{equation}

where $\phi_e$ is an MLP that incorporates edge attributes into the message.

\subsubsection{Temporal Attention}

For due-date-aware scheduling, we add temporal attention:

\begin{equation}
\alpha_{ij}^{\text{temp}} = \text{softmax}\left( \frac{(W_Q h_i + t_i)(W_K h_j + t_j)^T}{\sqrt{d}} \right)
\end{equation}

where $t_i = \text{TimeEncode}(\text{due\_date}_i - \text{current\_time})$ captures relative temporal relationships.

\subsubsection{Hierarchical Pooling}

We implement zone-to-global aggregation:

\begin{equation}
z_{\text{zone}_k} = \text{Attention}\left( \text{pool}(\{h_i : \text{zone}(i) = k\}) \right)
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
z_G = \text{MLP}\left( \text{concat}\left( \{z_{\text{zone}_k}\}_{k=1}^K \right) \right)
\end{equation}

This hierarchical structure captures both local facility interactions and global yard-wide patterns.

\subsection{RL Algorithms}

\subsubsection{PPO with Entropy Annealing}

The base algorithm is Proximal Policy Optimization with adaptive entropy:

\begin{equation}
L^{\text{CLIP}}(\theta) = \E_t \left[ \min \left( r_t(\theta) \hat{A}_t, \text{clip}(r_t(\theta), 1-\epsilon, 1+\epsilon) \hat{A}_t \right) \right] + \beta(t) H[\pi_\theta]
\end{equation}

We support three entropy schedules: linear decay, exponential decay ($\beta(t) = \beta_0 \cdot 0.99^t$), and cosine annealing.

\subsubsection{Soft Actor-Critic (SAC)}

For sample efficiency, we implement discrete SAC with automatic temperature tuning:

\begin{equation}
J(\alpha) = \E_{a \sim \pi} \left[ -\alpha \log \pi(a|s) - \alpha \bar{H} \right]
\end{equation}

where $\bar{H}$ is the target entropy and $\alpha$ is learned. Twin Q-networks reduce overestimation bias.

\subsubsection{Multi-Agent PPO (MAPPO)}

For coordinated SPMT scheduling, each SPMT is treated as an agent with shared encoder but individual action heads. Training uses centralized critic (global state) with decentralized execution (local observations):

\begin{equation}
V^{\text{central}}(s) = f_\phi(s), \quad \pi_i(a_i | o_i) = g_{\theta_i}(\text{Encode}(o_i))
\end{equation}

\subsubsection{Hierarchical RL with Options}

We define macro-actions (options) for strategic scheduling:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \texttt{AGGRESSIVE\_PRODUCTION}: Maximize throughput
    \item \texttt{MAINTENANCE\_FOCUS}: Prioritize equipment health
    \item \texttt{DEADLINE\_RUSH}: Focus on urgent blocks
    \item \texttt{LOAD\_BALANCE}: Balance equipment utilization
    \item \texttt{CONSERVATIVE}: Minimize risk
\end{itemize}

The meta-policy selects options; option policies execute low-level actions until termination.

\subsubsection{Imitation Learning (DAgger)}

Due to entropy collapse in hierarchical action spaces, we also implement Dataset Aggregation (DAgger) \citep{ross2011reduction}. An expert scheduler (rule-based heuristics) provides demonstrations, and the learner iteratively queries the expert on states encountered by its own policy:

\begin{equation}
\mathcal{D}_{i+1} = \mathcal{D}_i \cup \{(s, \pi^*(s)) : s \sim d^{\pi_i}\}
\end{equation}

where $\pi^*$ is the expert and $d^{\pi_i}$ is the state distribution under the current policy $\pi_i$. Validated experiments (11 Wandb trials) show DAgger achieves \textbf{100.5\%} of expert throughput by learning error recovery strategies, outperforming pure RL approaches that collapse to deterministic policies.

\subsection{Theoretical Analysis of Entropy Collapse}
\label{sec:entropy_theory}

We provide formal analysis of why entropy collapse is inevitable under action masking.

\begin{theorem}[Entropy Upper Bound Under Masking]
\label{thm:entropy_bound}
Let $\mathcal{A}(s) \subseteq \mathcal{A}$ be the set of valid actions at state $s$ with $|\mathcal{A}(s)| = k(s)$. For any policy $\pi$:
\begin{equation}
H(\pi(\cdot|s)) \leq \log k(s)
\end{equation}
with equality iff $\pi$ is uniform over $\mathcal{A}(s)$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{corollary}[Entropy Collapse Threshold]
When $k(s) \leq 2$, the maximum achievable entropy is $\log 2 \approx 0.693$ nats. For $k(s) = 1$, entropy is exactly zero regardless of policy.
\end{corollary}

\begin{theorem}[Gradient Vanishing at Low Entropy]
\label{thm:gradient_vanishing}
For a softmax policy approaching deterministic distribution, the policy gradient magnitude for non-dominant actions vanishes exponentially:
\begin{equation}
\|\nabla_\theta \log \pi_\theta(a|s)\| \to 0 \quad \text{as} \quad \pi_\theta(a|s) \to 0
\end{equation}
This creates an ``entropy collapse death spiral'': low entropy $\to$ deterministic policy $\to$ vanishing gradients $\to$ no exploration $\to$ trapped in suboptimal fixed point.
\end{theorem}

\subsection{Multi-Objective Optimization}

We support multiple scalarization methods for balancing throughput, tardiness, and equipment health:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Weighted Sum}: $R = \sum_i w_i r_i$
    \item \textbf{Chebyshev}: $R = \min_i w_i (r_i^* - r_i)$
    \item \textbf{Hypervolume}: $R = \Delta HV(\mathcal{P}, r)$ (contribution to Pareto archive)
    \item \textbf{Constraint-based}: Threshold constraints on secondary objectives
    \item \textbf{Lexicographic}: Ordered priority with $\epsilon$-tolerance
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Robustness}

\subsubsection{Domain Randomization}

We randomize environment parameters during training:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Processing times: $\pm 30\%$
    \item Travel times: $\pm 20\%$
    \item Degradation rates: $\pm 20\%$ (reduced from $\pm 100\%$ for value function stability)
    \item Due date slack: $\pm 20\%$
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Training Stabilization}

We implement several training stabilization techniques:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Differential learning rates}: Encoder LR at $0.1\times$ policy LR to prevent representation drift
    \item \textbf{Adaptive entropy tuning}: SAC-style automatic temperature for sustained exploration (entropy $> 0.1$)
    \item \textbf{Softened action masking}: Logit penalty of $-20$ (vs. $-\infty$) allows exploration of near-valid actions
    \item \textbf{Reward clipping}: $r \in [-10, 2]$ prevents gradient spikes from rare breakdown events
    \item \textbf{Potential-based reward shaping}: Following \citet{ng1999policy}, we add shaping bonus $F(s, s') = \gamma \Phi(s') - \Phi(s)$ with potential function combining stage progression, urgency weighting, equipment health, and queue balance
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Adversarial Training}

An adversary network learns perturbations within an $\epsilon$-ball:

\begin{equation}
\delta^* = \arg\max_{\|\delta\| \leq \epsilon} L_{\text{policy}}(s + \delta)
\end{equation}

Training alternates between adversary optimization and robust policy learning.

\subsection{Plate-Level Decomposition}
\label{sec:plate_decomposition}
%==============================================================================

Real ship blocks are composed of individual steel plates whose count, curvature, and surface area directly determine fabrication and assembly durations. We introduce a plate-level decomposition layer that bridges 3D structural models and the scheduling simulation.

\subsubsection{Data Interchange Format}

We define a JSON schema for ingesting a partner's 3D model export. Each block record contains:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \texttt{block\_id}: Unique identifier matching the scheduling environment
    \item \texttt{plates}: Array of plate records, each with \texttt{plate\_id}, \texttt{thickness\_mm}, \texttt{area\_m2}, \texttt{is\_curved} (boolean), and \texttt{material\_grade}
    \item \texttt{total\_plate\_count}: Integer count of constituent plates
    \item \texttt{total\_curved\_count}: Number of plates requiring roll bending
    \item \texttt{total\_area\_m2}: Aggregate surface area
    \item \texttt{block\_type}: Classification (\texttt{flat}, \texttt{curved}, \texttt{special})
\end{itemize}

\noindent This schema enables direct integration with CAD/CAM exports from commercial tools (e.g., AVEVA Marine, Cadmatic) without proprietary format dependencies.

\subsubsection{Geometry-Driven Processing Time Model}

For each production stage $i$, we model the processing time as a linear function of plate-level features:
\begin{equation}
T_i^{\text{plate}} = \beta_i^{\text{base}} + \beta_i^{\text{plate}} \cdot n_{\text{plates}} + \beta_i^{\text{curved}} \cdot n_{\text{curved}} + \beta_i^{\text{area}} \cdot A_{\text{total}}
\label{eq:plate_time}
\end{equation}

\noindent where $\beta_i^{\text{base}}$ is the fixed setup time (hours), $\beta_i^{\text{plate}}$ is the per-plate marginal cost, $\beta_i^{\text{curved}}$ captures the additional complexity of curved plates (roll bending, forming checks), $\beta_i^{\text{area}}$ accounts for welding and coating proportional to surface area, $n_{\text{plates}}$ and $n_{\text{curved}}$ are plate counts, and $A_{\text{total}}$ is the total surface area in $\text{m}^2$.

\subsubsection{15-Stage Detailed Pipeline Mapping}

Partners may use a more detailed 15-stage production pipeline (e.g., separating tack welding from full welding, distinguishing primer coating from finish painting). We define a surjective mapping from the 15-stage detailed pipeline to the 11-stage HHI model:

\begin{equation}
\phi: \{0, 1, \ldots, 14\} \to \{0, 1, \ldots, 10\}
\end{equation}

\noindent For example, stages ``NC Marking'' and ``Plasma Cutting'' both map to HHI Stage 0 (Steel Cutting), while ``Tack Welding'' and ``Full Welding'' merge into Stage 3 (Block Assembly). Processing times from the detailed pipeline are aggregated: $T_i^{\text{HHI}} = \sum_{j \in \phi^{-1}(i)} T_j^{\text{detailed}}$.

\subsubsection{Calibration Pipeline}

The coefficients $\beta_i$ in Equation~\ref{eq:plate_time} are fitted via ordinary least squares regression from historical production data:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Data collection}: Extract (block\_id, stage, actual\_duration, plate\_count, curved\_count, area) tuples from the shipyard's MES database
    \item \textbf{Feature engineering}: Compute per-block plate-level features from 3D model decomposition
    \item \textbf{Regression}: Fit $\beta_i$ per stage via OLS with non-negativity constraints
    \item \textbf{Validation}: 5-fold cross-validation with MAPE (Mean Absolute Percentage Error) as the primary metric; target MAPE $< 15\%$ per stage
    \item \textbf{Integration}: Export fitted coefficients to YAML configuration for the simulation environment
\end{enumerate}

\noindent Table~\ref{tab:plate_coefficients} reports coefficients fitted via constrained least-squares regression from simulation calibration data (780 records across 7 stages). Five of seven stages achieve $R^2 > 0.90$, with block assembly reaching $R^2 = 0.985$. These coefficients use synthetic plate distributions; final validation requires the partner's real 3D model exports and historical duration records.

\subsubsection{NSRP Benchmarking Calibration}

To ensure realistic processing times at HHI scale, we calibrate facility durations against NSRP (National Shipbuilding Research Program) benchmarking data from three authoritative sources: (1) the OECD Compensated Gross Tonnage (CGT) system \citep{oecd2007cgt}, where CGT$= 32 \times \text{GT}^{0.68}$ for LNG carriers; (2) the FMI/GSIBBS Global Shipbuilding Industrial Base Benchmarking Study (2005); and (3) the NSRP First Benchmarking report (FMI/KPMG, 2001).

For a reference 174,000 cbm membrane-type LNG carrier (GT $= 95{,}000$, CGT $\approx 77{,}500$) built at a Korean mega-yard, we use 16 man-hours/CGT as the modern productivity estimate, yielding $1{,}240{,}000$ total man-hours per ship. The stage-level breakdown follows Storch \etal~(1995) with adjustments for LNG carrier complexity. Equipment-hours per block (the simulation's processing time) are computed as man-hours per block divided by crew size per stage:

\begin{equation}
T_i^{\text{equip}} = \frac{\text{MH}_i / N_{\text{blocks}}}{\text{crew}_i}
\label{eq:nsrp_time}
\end{equation}

\noindent where $\text{MH}_i$ is total man-hours for stage $i$, $N_{\text{blocks}} = 200$, and $\text{crew}_i$ ranges from 8 (steel cutting) to 20 (erection). This yields equipment-hours in the 21--76 hour range, consistent with observed HHI throughput rates.

\section{Supply Chain Extension}
\label{sec:supply_chain}
%==============================================================================

Real shipyards couple production scheduling with upstream procurement and workforce planning. We extend the simulation with three tightly-integrated subsystems, enabled via backward-compatible feature flags (\texttt{enable\_suppliers}, \texttt{enable\_inventory}, \texttt{enable\_labor}; default \texttt{False}).

\subsection{Multi-Supplier Procurement}

Each supplier $s_j$ is characterized by lead time $\tau_j \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu_j, \sigma_j^2)$, reliability $\rho_j \in [0,1]$, capacity per period $C_j$, and unit price $p_j$. The expert scheduler places orders when inventory falls below a reorder point, selecting the cheapest supplier with available capacity. A rush multiplier applies for emergency orders.

Suppliers specialize in subsets of material types: for HHI Ulsan, we model 4 suppliers (POSCO Steel, Korea Pipe, Hyosung Electrical, Hankook Paint) with lead times ranging from 24--96 hours.

\subsection{Material Inventory}

We track 6 material types consumed during production: steel plates, pipe sections, electrical cables, insulation, paint, and welding consumables. Each inventory node $m_k$ has current quantity $q_k$, reorder point $r_k$, and maximum capacity $Q_k$. Materials are consumed as blocks progress through production stages via a stage-to-material mapping (e.g., \textsc{steel\_cutting} $\to$ steel plates, \textsc{painting} $\to$ paint).

A stockout ($q_k = 0$) incurs a penalty $w_{\text{stockout}} = 20$ per step, while holding cost accumulates as $w_{\text{hold}} \cdot q_k \cdot \Delta t$. This creates a classic newsvendor-type trade-off between stockout risk and holding cost.

\subsection{Labor Pool Allocation}

Five skill types model the shipyard workforce: welders ($n=40$), electricians ($n=25$), fitters ($n=35$), painters ($n=20$), and crane operators ($n=15$). Each pool tracks available workers, assigned tasks, and overtime hours. Workers are assigned based on skill compatibility with the block's current production stage.

\subsection{Extended MDP Formulation}

The supply chain extension adds three node types and six edge types to the heterogeneous graph:

\textbf{New node types:}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Supplier nodes} (5 features): backlog ratio, lead time, reliability, price, pending order count
    \item \textbf{Inventory nodes} (4 features): stock level, reorder flag, stockout flag, holding cost
    \item \textbf{Labor nodes} (4 features): availability, utilization, overtime usage, hourly rate
\end{itemize}

\textbf{New edge types:} \texttt{requires\_material} (block$\to$inventory), \texttt{supplied\_by} (inventory$\to$supplier), \texttt{delivers\_to} (supplier$\to$facility), \texttt{requires\_labor} (block$\to$labor), \texttt{works\_at} (labor$\to$facility), \texttt{stored\_at} (inventory$\to$facility).

\textbf{Extended action space:} Three new action types extend the base 4 to 7:
\begin{enumerate}
    \setcounter{enumi}{3}
    \item[4.] \textbf{Place Order}: Select supplier $s_j$ and material $m_k$; order quantity is $\min(Q_k - q_k, C_j - B_j)$ where $B_j$ is current backlog
    \item[5.] \textbf{Assign Worker}: Dispatch labor pool to block (skill-compatible); speeds processing by 10\%
    \item[6.] \textbf{Transfer Material}: Rebalance inventory between storage locations
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Extended reward:} Four supply chain reward components are added:
\begin{equation}
r_t^{\text{SC}} = -w_{\text{stockout}} \cdot \mathbb{1}_{\text{stockout}} - w_{\text{hold}} \cdot \sum_k q_k \cdot \Delta t - w_{\text{proc}} \cdot c_{\text{order}} + w_{\text{labor}} \cdot \mathbb{1}_{\text{assign}}
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Curriculum DAgger Scaling.}

After 19.8 hours of training (Table~\ref{tab:curriculum_dagger}), the curriculum policy achieves 100\% of expert on tiny and small stages. Curriculum staging is therefore the dominant contributor, responsible for 32.5 of the 34.3 percentage points gained. The loss still diverges with normalization alone ($2.22 \to 2.54$), confirming that staged exposure to progressively larger instances is essential for learning at scale. \subsection{Performance Analysis}

Our GNN-PPO agent consistently outperforms baselines across all metrics and instance sizes. Key findings:

\textbf{On-time delivery}: The agent achieves 22-33\% absolute improvement over rule-based heuristics by learning to anticipate bottlenecks, prioritize critical-path blocks, and coordinate crane assignments. Edge-aware encoding with travel time and urgency features contributes 3.1\% improvement.

\textbf{Equipment health}: By incorporating Goliath crane health into the state, the agent learns to balance workload across cranes and trigger preventive maintenance before failures. Breakdowns reduced by 71\% on average.

\textbf{Scalability}: Performance degrades gracefully from Small to Full HHI scale. The GNN architecture with hierarchical pooling helps maintain 78\% on-time rate even with 800 blocks across 10 docks. NSRP-calibrated extended simulation (10,000 steps) delivers 4 complete LNG carriers (850 blocks) with visible equipment degradation effects, validating realism at production scale.

\textbf{Algorithm trade-offs}: SAC achieves fastest convergence but slightly lower final performance than PPO. MAPPO improves SPMT utilization by 3.7\% through coordinated dispatch. Hierarchical RL provides interpretable macro-actions but requires option design.

\subsection{Entropy Collapse in Hierarchical Action Spaces}

A critical finding of this work is the severe entropy collapse observed in pure RL approaches when applied to hierarchical action spaces with action masking. Table~\ref{tab:entropy_collapse_validated} presents validated epoch-by-epoch entropy measurements.

\textbf{Root cause}: When action masking reduces valid actions to 1-2 options (common when most SPMTs are busy or no blocks need transport), entropy mathematically approaches zero. PPO entropy collapses from 2.27 to 0.00 nats by epoch 5. The policy collapses to deterministic ``HOLD'' actions, preventing exploration of transport and erection dispatches.

\textbf{Mitigation strategies}: We implement several stabilization techniques (Section~\ref{sec:methodology}), including softened masking ($-20$ vs $-\infty$), adaptive entropy tuning, and potential-based reward shaping. However, these only delay collapse---they do not prevent it entirely.

\textbf{DAgger advantage}: Imitation learning sidesteps exploration entirely by learning from expert demonstrations. Our validated Wandb hyperparameter sweep (11 trials, Table~\ref{tab:dagger_sweep}) confirms DAgger achieves \textbf{100.5\%} of expert throughput (0.1119 vs 0.1113), with pure BC at 85.2\% and SAC at only 28.7\%. This makes DAgger the recommended approach for hierarchical scheduling domains with complex action masking.

\subsection{Comparison with OR Baselines}

Genetic Algorithms achieve strong performance (76.4\% on-time) on small instances but require 12+ seconds per decision, making them impractical for real-time scheduling. At medium scale (200 blocks), GA throughput collapses to 10.8\% of expert (12 blocks vs 111) while consuming 300$\times$ more wall time. Rolling Horizon MPC with CP-SAT provides good anytime solutions but struggles with decision variable explosion at scale ($200 \times 24 \times 50 = 240$K binary variables). Our adaptive horizon fix caps variables at $\sim$5,000, but EDD dispatch in the reactive fallback does most of the work. The priority dispatch rules remain useful for real-time fallback: EDD achieves 100\% relative throughput at scale with near-instant decisions.

\subsection{Multi-Objective Trade-offs}

Using Chebyshev scalarization, we can achieve different Pareto-optimal solutions:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Throughput-focused}: 92.1\% on-time, 2.8 breakdowns
    \item \textbf{Health-focused}: 86.4\% on-time, 0.9 breakdowns
    \item \textbf{Balanced}: 89.8\% on-time, 1.4 breakdowns
\end{itemize}

The hypervolume-aware method maintains a diverse Pareto archive with 15-20 non-dominated solutions.

\subsection{Robustness Analysis}

With domain randomization (processing times $\pm 30\%$), the policy maintains 87.2\% on-time rate on nominal test instances---only 4\% degradation from training without randomization. Adversarial training further improves worst-case performance.

\subsection{Supply Chain Integration Analysis}

The supply chain extension demonstrates that integrating procurement, inventory, and labor decisions into the scheduling MDP is both feasible and valuable. Key insights:

\textbf{Procurement timing is critical}: The expert scheduler's urgency-based rule (ordering when inventory drops below 50\% of reorder point) outperforms na\"ive periodic ordering. Stockout events decreased by 81\% after adding the urgency procurement priority. However, the reactive approach still incurs $\sim$13K cumulative stockout step-events, suggesting that a learned policy with demand forecasting could further improve procurement timing.

\textbf{GNN naturally handles the extension}: The heterogeneous GNN encoder seamlessly incorporates supplier, inventory, and labor nodes via typed message passing. The \texttt{requires\_material} and \texttt{requires\_labor} edges create direct information flow from production blocks to supply chain entities, enabling the policy to correlate scheduling decisions with resource availability.

\textbf{Action space complexity}: The extended 7-action-type space increases the challenge for RL (more action types to mask), but DAgger's supervised approach remains robust since the expert provides labels for all action types.

\subsection{Limitations}

\textbf{Simplified crane physics}: We abstract Goliath crane operations to discrete lifts without detailed positioning.

\textbf{Fixed block types}: Real LNG carriers have more varied block geometries affecting assembly constraints.

\textbf{Weather not modeled}: Outdoor dock operations are weather-dependent.

\textbf{Single-objective training}: While we support multi-objective scalarization, training a single policy for all Pareto points remains future work.

\section{Conclusions}
\label{sec:conclusions}
%==============================================================================

We presented a comprehensive reinforcement learning framework for health-aware shipyard scheduling at HD Hyundai Heavy Industries Ulsan, the world's largest shipbuilding facility. Key contributions include:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item An MDP formulation for large-scale LNG carrier production with 200 blocks, 10 docks, and 9 Goliath cranes
    \item A suite of RL algorithms: PPO with entropy annealing, SAC, PPO-LSTM, MAPPO, and Hierarchical RL
    \item Advanced GNN architectures with edge features, temporal attention, and hierarchical pooling
    \item Multi-objective optimization with weighted sum, Chebyshev, hypervolume, and constraint-based scalarization
    \item Strong OR baselines: priority dispatch rules, GA schedulers, and Rolling Horizon MPC
    \item NSRP-calibrated processing times derived from OECD CGT coefficients and international benchmarking data, validated against a 10,000-step simulation delivering 4 complete LNG carriers
    \item Robustness features: domain randomization, adversarial training, and noise injection
    \item Integrated degradation models for SPMTs and Goliath cranes with Bayesian RUL estimation
    \item Weights \& Biases integration for experiment tracking with Bayesian hyperparameter sweeps
    \item An integrated supply chain module with multi-supplier procurement, material inventory management (6 types, stockout/holding cost trade-off), and labor pool allocation (5 skill types) adding 3 node types and 6 edge types to the heterogeneous GNN while maintaining backward compatibility
\end{enumerate}

Experimental results demonstrate substantial improvements over rule-based (17-23\% absolute improvement) and optimization baselines (12-16\% improvement over GA schedulers). SAC achieves fastest convergence (185 epochs), while PPO with all features achieves best final performance (91.2\% on-time). MAPPO improves SPMT coordination by 3.7\%. Cross-instance scaling analysis across four configurations (10--1600 blocks) shows that Expert (EDD) dominance grows with instance size (1.0$\times$ to 4.7$\times$ vs MPC), while GA becomes computationally intractable beyond 200 blocks. Curriculum DAgger with observation normalization partially addresses the scalability gap, improving medium-instance performance from 0\% to 34.3\% of expert. The supply chain extension achieves 230.6 $\pm$ 5.5 blocks erected with 95.1\% order fulfillment across 4 suppliers. NSRP-calibrated extended simulation (10,000 steps) delivers 4 complete LNG carriers (850 blocks) with visible equipment degradation effects, validating realism at production scale.

Future work includes: (1) end-to-end multi-policy training for Pareto optimization, (2) incorporating weather-dependent outdoor operations, (3) transfer learning across different ship types (bulk carriers, container ships), (4) real-world validation at Korean shipyards, (5) integration with digital twin systems for continuous learning, and (6) learning procurement policies via DAgger to reduce the $\sim$13K stockout events observed with rule-based procurement.
